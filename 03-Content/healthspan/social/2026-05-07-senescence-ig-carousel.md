### Slide 2 — What Is a Senescent Cell?

**Headline:**
What is a senescent cell?

**Body:**
A cell that permanently stops dividing — but doesn't die.

Instead of being cleared, it stays in your tissue and releases toxic signals that damage everything around it.

Scientists call them "zombie cells."

Your immune system is supposed to clear them. As you age, it can't keep up.

---

### Slide 3 — What Triggers Senescence?

**Headline:**
What causes cells to go senescent?

**Body:**
Four main triggers:

→ **Telomere shortening** — cells can only divide so many times before their chromosomes wear down
→ **DNA damage** — from oxidative stress, radiation, or environmental toxins
→ **Oncogene activation** — the body's built-in cancer brake
→ **Chemotherapy & radiation** — therapeutic senescence in surrounding tissue

Each trigger is the cell's protective response. The problem is the long-term accumulation.

---

### Slide 4 — The SASP

**Headline:**
The real problem: the SASP

**Body:**
Senescent cells don't just sit there quietly.

They secrete a toxic mixture of inflammatory proteins called the **Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype (SASP).**

The SASP drives:
→ Chronic, systemic inflammation
→ Breakdown of surrounding tissue structure
→ Neighboring healthy cells becoming senescent
→ Immune confusion — the cells evade clearance

This is "inflammaging." It underlies nearly every age-related disease.

---

### Slide 5 — Why They Accumulate With Age

**Headline:**
Why aging = more zombie cells

**Body:**
In your 20s and 30s, your immune system clears senescent cells efficiently — before they can build up.

As you age, immune function declines. (This is called immunosenescence — yes, immune cells go senescent too.)

The result: senescent cells accumulate — especially in fat tissue, the liver, lungs, kidneys, and brain.

The SASP compounds. Inflammation becomes baseline.

---

### Slide 6 — What Science Can Do About It

**Headline:**
Two evidence-based approaches

**Body:**
**Senolytics** — selectively clear senescent cells
→ Fisetin (natural polyphenol, studied at Mayo Clinic)
→ Dasatinib + Quercetin (most studied human combination)

**Senostatics** — suppress the SASP without clearing the cell
→ Rapamycin (via mTOR inhibition — the same mechanism that extends lifespan in animal models)

These aren't theoretical. Both classes have published animal lifespan data and early-stage human trials.
