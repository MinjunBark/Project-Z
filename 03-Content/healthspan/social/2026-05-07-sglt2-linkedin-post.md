SGLT2 inhibitors were designed for type 2 diabetes.

What we didn't expect was what they were doing to everything else.

---

I've been watching the longevity literature on SGLT2 inhibitors for several years now. What started as a class of glucose-lowering drugs has become, in my view, one of the most pharmacologically interesting longevity interventions currently available — and one of the most underappreciated.

Here's what changed my thinking.

**The EMPA-REG anomaly**

In 2015, the EMPA-REG OUTCOME trial published results that stopped cardiologists cold. Empagliflozin — an SGLT2 inhibitor — reduced cardiovascular mortality in diabetic patients by 38%. Heart failure hospitalizations dropped by 35%.

The effect appeared within the first few months of treatment.

That timing matters. Atherosclerotic plaque doesn't regress in months. Blood vessel remodeling doesn't happen in months. Whatever empagliflozin was doing, it was not simply improving glucose control and waiting for downstream benefits.

Something else was happening at the cellular level.

**The mechanism that caught our attention**

SGLT2 inhibitors activate AMPK — AMP-activated protein kinase — the body's master energy sensor. When AMPK is active, it behaves similarly to caloric restriction: it promotes autophagy, improves mitochondrial efficiency, and suppresses mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin).

That last point is significant. Chronic mTOR activation is one of the most consistently replicated drivers of biological aging. It's the primary target of rapamycin — the compound with the strongest lifespan extension data in animal models. SGLT2 inhibitors suppress mTOR through AMPK, arriving at a similar destination through a different mechanism.

There's more.

By lowering circulating glucose and suppressing insulin, SGLT2 inhibitors induce mild ketosis. Beta-hydroxybutyrate — the primary ketone body — isn't just a fuel source. It inhibits the NLRP3 inflammasome, one of the central drivers of chronic inflammatory signaling. It functions as an HDAC inhibitor, influencing gene expression in patterns associated with longevity. The anti-inflammatory effects of SGLT2 inhibitors appear to operate through at least two independent pathways simultaneously.

**What the aging science added**

The National Institute on Aging Interventions Testing Program — the most rigorous preclinical longevity testing program in the United States — tests compounds for lifespan extension in genetically heterogeneous mice. Most compounds fail.

Canagliflozin, an SGLT2 inhibitor, extended median lifespan in male mice significantly. This places it in a short list of compounds with replicated longevity data in a well-controlled setting.

The ITP result doesn't prove human lifespan extension. But combined with the AMPK/mTOR mechanism, the cardiovascular outcome data, and the anti-inflammatory effects, a coherent biological picture emerges: SGLT2 inhibitors may be doing something meaningful at the level of aging biology — not just glucose management.

**The clinical question this raises**

The diabetes trials enrolled diabetic patients. The cardiovascular benefits were observed in that population. We don't yet have large-scale, long-duration randomized trials in non-diabetic patients with longevity as a primary endpoint.

That's the honest caveat.

But the mechanism doesn't care about the patient's diagnosis. AMPK activation, mTOR suppression, and NLRP3 inhibition are not diabetes-specific pathways. They are fundamental regulators of cellular aging that operate across metabolic states.

At Healthspan, we don't prescribe SGLT2 inhibitors to everyone. We use them as one component of individualized protocols — designed after 70+ biomarker panels establish a patient's metabolic, inflammatory, and hormonal baseline. For patients where the risk-benefit analysis supports it, the longevity rationale is now part of the clinical conversation alongside the cardiometabolic rationale.

**Why this matters for how we think about "longevity drugs"**

For years, the longevity medicine conversation centered almost entirely on rapamycin and metformin. Both are legitimate — the evidence base for each is substantial.

SGLT2 inhibitors deserve a seat at that table.

They have something rapamycin and metformin don't: large-scale human cardiovascular outcome data showing dramatic reductions in mortality. That's not a surrogate endpoint. Those are real deaths, in real patients, not occurring.

The longevity interpretation of that finding is still being worked out by the research community. But the signal is too consistent, and the mechanism too well-understood, to dismiss.

We're watching this class of drugs closely. And in the right patient profile, we're already using them.
